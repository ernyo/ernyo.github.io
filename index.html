What Is Federated Learning?

Federated Learning is a decentralized machine learning paradigm proposed by Google to address data isolation and privacy concerns in large-scale, data-driven systems. Unlike centralized learning, where data is collected and stored in a single repository, Federated Learning trains models directly on clients’ local datasets and exchanges only model parameters or updates for server-side aggregation.

By keeping sensitive data decentralized, Federated Learning significantly reduces privacy and security risks while enabling collaborative model training across parties, which is crucial for privacy-sensitive domains such as healthcare, transportation, and finance. A notable example is the collaborative development of AI models for COVID-19 diagnosis from medical imaging data across institutions worldwide, where Federated Learning enabled effective model training while preserving patient privacy.

What Is Federated Multi-Task Learning?

Local datasets often exhibit substantial statistical heterogeneity due to differences in population characteristics, data collection procedures, and data quantity or quality, which can significantly degrade convergence and generalization in Federated Learning. Moreover, task heterogeneity occurs as clients may not want to optimize their models for the same objectives due to variations in application requirements or task definitions, limiting the suitability of a single global model.

Rather than producing one global model, Federated Multi-Task Learning aims to learn client-specific models that preserve task-level differences while still enabling cross-client knowledge transfer by capturing shared structure across clients. This is particularly relevant in settings where client data is limited or highly specialized, as leveraging shared information can improve learning efficiency and robustness.

How Does Training Work?

In a standard client-server Federated Multi-Task Learning setting with \(N\) clients, each client \(C_i\) maintains model parameters \(\theta_i\) and trains locally on its private dataset. Training proceeds for \(R\) communication rounds. In each round, model parameters of previous round \(\theta^{(r-1)}\) and the updates in current round \(\Delta\theta^{(r)}\) from all clients are sent to the server for aggregation. After each training round, client-level and server-level metrics are collected and processed for visualization.

Pseudocode:


        Server Update
        1  for each communication round \( r \in \{1,\ldots,R\} \) do
        2    for each client \( C_i \) do
        3      \( \Delta\theta_i^{(r)} \leftarrow \mathrm{ClientUpdate}\!\left(\theta_i^{(r-1)}\right) \)
        4    end for
        5    Server gathers updates of client models \( \Delta\theta^{(r)} \)
        6    \( \theta^{(r)} \leftarrow \mathrm{Aggregation}\!\left(\theta^{(r-1)}, \Delta\theta^{(r)}\right) \)
        7  end for

        Client Update\( \left(\theta_i^{(r-1)}\right) \)
        1  \( \theta_i \leftarrow \theta_i^{(r-1)} \)
        2  for each local epoch \( e \in \{1,\ldots,E\} \) do
        3    for batch \( B_i \) do
        4      Compute loss \( L_i=\sum_{t\in T_i}\lambda_t\,L_i^t(\theta_i;B_i) \)
        5      Update \( \theta_i \leftarrow \theta_i-\eta\,\nabla_{\theta_i}L_i \)
        6    end for
        7  end for
        8  return \( \Delta\theta_i^{(r)}=\theta_i-\theta_i^{(r-1)} \)

What Aggregation Algorithms Are Available?

The most common Federated Learning algorithm is FedAvg. In each training round, clients’ updated parameters are collected by the server after their respective local training, which computes a data-size-weighted average to form the next global model:

\[ \theta^{(r)}=\sum_{k\in S_r}\frac{s_k}{\sum_j s_j}\,\theta_k^{(r)} \]

where \(s_k\) is the number of samples used by client \(k\) during local training, and \(\theta_k^{(r)}\) denotes the client’s model parameters after local training.

FedAvg performs best when the clients have similar data distributions and task setups.

In contrast, FedHCA² is a framework specifically designed to address data and task heterogeneity by disassembling client models into encoders and decoders for decoupled aggregation and redistributing the updates to each client’s model.

For encoders, Hyper Conflict-Averse Aggregation is used to alleviate update conflicts among clients by finding an optimal aggregated encoder update direction \(\tilde{U}\) to mitigate the negative effects of conflicting parameter updates:

\[ \tilde{U}=\bar{\Delta\theta}^{E}+\frac{\sqrt{\Phi}}{\lVert U_w\rVert}\,U_w \] \[ U_w=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i\,\Delta\theta_i^{E} \qquad \Phi=c^2\left\lVert \bar{\Delta\theta}^{E}\right\rVert^2 \]

For decoders, Hyper Cross Attention Aggregation performs layer-wise attention-weighted aggregation to capture both the commonalities and discrepancies among tasks, allowing for fine-grained personalized aggregation that alleviates model incongruity:

\[ \tilde{\theta}_i^{D}=\sum_{j=1}^{N}\gamma_{i,j}\,\theta_j^{D} \] \[ V_l=\left[\Delta\theta_{1,l}^{D},\ldots,\Delta\theta_{K,l}^{D}\right]^{\top} \] \[ \tilde{A}_{i,l}=\mathrm{Softmax}\!\left(\frac{\Delta\theta_{i,l}^{D} V_l^{\top}}{\sqrt{d}}\right)V_l \]

where \(\Delta\theta_{i,l}^{D}\) and \(\tilde{A}_{i,l}\) are the original update and aggregated update for the \(l\)-th layer of the \(i\)-th decoder, with dimension \(d\).

FedHCA² performs better than FedAvg in heterogeneous settings, especially when clients have significantly different data distributions and tasks.
